About this site

Built by a student.
For students.

My name is Caleb. I'm a junior in Texas with ADHD, anxiety, and fine motor delay. I built this because I needed it and couldn't find it.

The story

How AccessEd started

At least once a year, I sat in IEP meetings with school administrators and staff without really knowing what was going on. I smiled and nodded along. I signed documents I didn't fully understand. I accepted what I was told.

At one point, administrators told me I didn't need certain accommodations because I was doing well in school. I believed them.

When I told my parents, they told me that wasn't okay. So I went down a rabbit hole.

What I found online was overwhelming. Massive legal documents. Forms written for lawyers. Vague school district policy pages that never just said what I needed to know. I didn't have it in me to spend hours reading things that stressed me out. I just wanted someone to explain it clearly.

What I eventually learned changed everything. Teachers cannot take your accommodations away because you're doing well in school. Your grades do not determine your rights. And the list of accommodations you can ask for is much longer than anyone ever told me.

I built AccessEd because I know what it feels like to sit in that room and not understand what's happening. I wanted to make the thing I needed but couldn't find. Something written for a student like me, not for lawyers or administrators.

What this site is

Free. Student-written. Based on real law.

AccessEd is a free educational resource about disability rights and school accommodations. Here's what that actually means.

  • ✓ Free. There is nothing to buy, no account to create, and no paywall. Everything here is free to read and use.
  • ✓ Student-written. This site was built by a student with disabilities, for students with disabilities. Not by a law firm. Not by a school district. Not by a government agency.
  • ✓ Based on real federal law. Everything here comes from IDEA, Section 504, and the ADA. The laws are real. Your rights under them are real.
  • ✓ Written in plain English. No legal jargon. No 80-page PDFs. No forms written for parents and lawyers. Just what you need to know, explained like a person is talking to you.
  • ✓ Independent. AccessEd is not affiliated with any school, government agency, or legal organization.

What this site is not

This site has real limits.

A few things to be upfront about before you rely on this.

This is not legal advice. Nothing on this site is legal advice. It is educational information about federal law, written in plain English. Reading this site does not make us have any kind of professional relationship.

This was not written by a lawyer. It was written by a high school student in Texas who has ADHD, anxiety, and fine motor delay. I tried to get it right, but I am not a licensed attorney.

If you have a serious dispute with your school, this site is not enough on its own. Contact a disability rights attorney, a Parent Training and Information Center, or a Protection and Advocacy organization in your state. The Resources page has links to all of these. They exist to help you.

Laws and policies can change. State laws and district policies vary. Always verify important details with official sources before making decisions based on what you read here.
