What changes when you graduate

College is different.
Start preparing now.

The protections you have in K-12 don't automatically follow you to college. The laws change, the process changes, and the burden shifts to you. Here's everything you need to know before you go.

The biggest misconception

"My IEP will follow me to college."

It won't. And this surprises more students than almost anything else about the transition to college.

Your IEP is an IDEA document. IDEA ends at high school graduation. The moment you walk across that stage, your IEP is no longer legally enforceable. It doesn't transfer to any college or university. The accommodations listed in it are not automatically granted. And no one at your college will reach out to ask if you need support.

The same is true of 504 Plans, with one difference: Section 504 and the ADA do still apply in college. But the way they work is completely different, and the burden shifts from the school to you.

IEPs end permanently at high school graduation. There is no college IEP. IDEA is a K-12 law. Once you graduate, you are no longer protected by it; you're protected by Section 504 and the ADA only.

College accommodations do not renew automatically each semester. At most colleges, you must re-request your accommodations every semester. If you miss the deadline, you may not receive them for that term.

How it changes

K-12 vs. college: what's different

The shift from high school to college is bigger than most people realize. Here's a side-by-side comparison.

Category K-12 College
Governing law IDEA, Section 504, ADA Section 504 and ADA only
Who identifies you School is responsible for finding students who need support (Child Find) You must identify yourself to disability services
Evaluation School provides free evaluations You must provide documentation (usually at your own cost)
Right to FAPE Yes: your school must provide a free, appropriate education No; college is not required to ensure your success, only equal access
Plan document IEP or 504 Plan, maintained by the school Accommodation letter issued by disability services office
Parent involvement Parents have extensive rights and participate in planning Unless you authorize it, the college communicates with you, not your parents
Accommodation delivery School staff implement your accommodations You deliver your accommodation letter to each professor each semester
Who initiates support School has a legal obligation to act You must register, request, and renew; no one will come looking for you

A gap most students don't know about

The documentation disconnect

When you register with your college's disability services office, they will ask you for documentation of your disability. Here's the problem: your high school IEP alone is often not enough.

Most colleges want current documentation from a licensed professional (a psychologist, neuropsychologist, or physician) that describes your disability, how it's diagnosed, and how it affects your functioning. An IEP is an educational plan, not a clinical document. Some colleges will accept it; many won't, or will only accept it alongside other documentation.

This catches students off guard at orientation, when it's too late to do anything about it. The fix is to plan ahead:

  • Get an updated neuropsychological evaluation before you graduate if possible, especially if your last one was more than 3 years ago.
  • Ask your school for documentation from the professionals who evaluated you (psychologist, speech therapist, etc.).
  • Request letters from providers who know your disability history.
  • Ask colleges you're considering what documentation they require before you apply; requirements vary significantly.

Don't wait until the summer before college. Getting updated evaluations takes time and can be expensive. Start looking into it in 10th or 11th grade if you plan to go to college.

Something most students never hear about

The Summary of Performance

IEP students only

What is the Summary of Performance?

The Summary of Performance (SOP) is a document that your school is required to provide you when your IEP ends, which happens at graduation or when you age out. It's a written summary of your academic achievement, functional performance, and recommendations for how to support your needs in postsecondary settings.

Most students have never heard of it. Your school is required to give it to you.

The SOP can be valuable when registering with college disability services: it documents your disability history in a way that may satisfy some of a college's documentation requirements, especially when combined with other records.

What to do: Before you graduate, ask your case manager or special education coordinator for your Summary of Performance. Ask them to make sure it's thorough and includes specific information about your functional limitations and the accommodations that helped you. Keep it with your other important documents.

The SOP requirement is under IDEA (20 U.S.C. § 1414(c)(5)(B)(ii)). Your school must provide it, but they don't always bring it up. You may need to ask.

Your pre-graduation checklist

What to do before you graduate

The earlier you do these things, the smoother your college transition will be.

  1. 1
    Request your Summary of Performance Ask your case manager before your senior year. Ask that it be detailed and include specific information about your functional limitations and the accommodations that have helped you.
  2. 2
    Get copies of all your evaluation reports Every psychoeducational evaluation, speech-language report, occupational therapy report, and other assessment should be in your files. Request copies. These are your records; you're entitled to them.
  3. 3
    Ask about updated evaluations If your last neuropsychological or psychoeducational evaluation is more than three years old, consider getting an updated one before you graduate. Talk to your parents and case manager about options: school, private evaluator, or community resources.
  4. 4
    Research what each college requires Look up the disability services office at every college you're seriously considering. Find out what documentation they require and what accommodations they commonly provide. Some colleges have more robust programs than others.
  5. 5
    Practice advocating for yourself In college, you're on your own. No parent in the meeting, no case manager sending reminders. Use your junior and senior years to practice talking directly to teachers and administrators about your needs. It's a skill, and it gets easier with practice.

Once you get to college

How to register with college disability services

Every college that receives federal funding must have a disability services office (sometimes called the Office of Accessibility, Student Accessibility Services, or similar). Here's how the process typically works.

  1. 1
    Register as early as possible, ideally before the semester starts Don't wait until you're struggling. Registering in the summer before your first semester means your accommodations are in place from day one.
  2. 2
    Submit your documentation Bring your evaluation reports, Summary of Performance, and any other relevant documentation. The disability services office will review it to determine what accommodations are appropriate.
  3. 3
    Meet with a disability services counselor You'll typically have an intake meeting to discuss your needs and what accommodations are available. This is your chance to explain how your disability affects you in an academic setting; be specific.
  4. 4
    Receive and deliver your accommodation letter each semester Most colleges issue a formal letter you give to each professor. You must deliver this letter yourself every semester. It doesn't go out automatically. If you forget, you may not receive your accommodations.
  5. 5
    Renew each semester At most colleges, accommodations don't roll over automatically. Check your disability services office's renewal process and deadlines at the start of every semester.

This is not legal advice. College accommodation processes vary by institution. Contact your specific college's disability services office for their exact requirements and deadlines.
