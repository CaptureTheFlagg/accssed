Step by step

There's a process.
Here's how it works.

Getting accommodations isn't random; there's a defined process with real deadlines. Once you submit a written request, the clock starts and your school must act. Here's exactly what happens.

Step zero

It starts with a written request

Written request = the official starting gun. Verbal conversations, phone calls, and hallway chats don't count. Once you (or a parent) submit a written request for an evaluation, your school is legally obligated to respond. Send it via email or certified letter. Keep a copy.

Your request doesn't need to be formal or use legal language. It just needs to be in writing and ask for an evaluation. Something as simple as: "I am requesting that [School Name] evaluate my child for a disability that may be affecting their education." That's enough to start the process. Get a sample letter on the Resources page.

Important: You do NOT need a diagnosis to request an evaluation. The school evaluates you; you don't have to come in with paperwork from a doctor. Asking for an evaluation is your right regardless of whether you've been diagnosed.

The IEP path

The IEP process, step by step

If you're seeking an IEP under IDEA, here's exactly what happens, and when.

Timeline at a glance

School receives your written request Day 0
School must decide whether to evaluate (and notify you in writing) Varies by state
Evaluation must be complete (from parent/guardian consent) 60 calendar days Federal baseline; your state may have a shorter timeline.
IEP must be developed after evaluation complete 30 days
IEP annual review required Every year
Reevaluation required Every 3 years
1
You do this

Submit a written request for evaluation

Put your request in writing and send it to your school's principal or special education coordinator. Email counts; keep a copy. State that you're requesting an evaluation under IDEA for a suspected disability affecting your education.

Tip: Note the date you sent it. Everything that follows is measured from this date.

2
School does this

School decides whether to evaluate

Your school must respond to your request in writing. They can either agree to evaluate or decline, but if they decline, they must give you a written explanation and tell you about your right to challenge the decision.

If they agree: They'll send you a written notice and a consent form. You (or a parent/guardian) must sign the consent form before the evaluation can begin. The 60-day evaluation clock starts when they receive your signed consent.

3
60 calendar days

School completes the evaluation

Your school has 60 calendar days from the date they receive your signed consent to complete a comprehensive evaluation. This is a federal timeline under IDEA; some states have shorter timelines, but none can be longer.

The evaluation is free and must be conducted by qualified professionals. It may include academic testing, psychological evaluation, observation in class, and input from your teachers.

Note: Some states have a preliminary step where the school decides whether to evaluate, which has its own timeline. Check your state's specific rules.

4
30 days after evaluation

IEP meeting and development

After the evaluation is complete, the school has 30 days to hold an IEP meeting and develop your IEP. This meeting must include: a parent/guardian, a general education teacher, a special education teacher, a school administrator, and any relevant specialists.

You can also attend your own IEP meeting. You should attend. This is where decisions about your education are made, and your voice matters.

The IEP document must include: your current level of performance, annual goals, specific services and accommodations, how progress will be measured, and how much time you'll spend in general education.

5
School does this

IEP is implemented

Once the IEP is finalized and signed, the school must begin implementing it as soon as possible. All your teachers should receive a copy (or at least know what's in it). If a teacher says they haven't seen your IEP, that's a problem, and you can report it.

Keep your own copy. If your accommodations aren't being followed, having the document in hand makes it much easier to advocate for yourself.

6
Ongoing

Annual review and triennial reevaluation

Your school must review your IEP at least once a year. At the annual review, your goals and services can be adjusted. You and your parents have the right to request a review at any time; you don't have to wait for the annual meeting.

Every three years, your school must conduct a full reevaluation to confirm you still qualify for special education services. You can decline the reevaluation if you already have sufficient data.

The 504 path

How 504 Plans work differently

The 504 process is similar in outline, but with fewer hard federal deadlines and less formal structure.

504 Plan process

Like an IEP, it starts with a written request for evaluation. But Section 504 has no fixed federal timeline; the law says your school must act in a "reasonable time." In practice, many districts use a 30-day standard for each step, but this isn't required by federal law.

Your school must still evaluate you, determine eligibility, and document your accommodations. The evaluation doesn't have to be as comprehensive as an IEP evaluation, and the resulting plan doesn't have to meet the same detailed federal requirements.

What this means for you: If your school is dragging its feet on a 504, you have less leverage on specific deadlines, but you still have the right to an evaluation and a response. Document everything, follow up in writing, and escalate if needed.

Some states have adopted their own 504 timelines. Check your state's department of education for specifics.

If things go wrong

What to do if your school misses a deadline

Schools sometimes miss timelines, intentionally or not. Here's how to escalate, in order.

This is not legal advice. These steps are general guidance. Every situation is different. For serious disputes, contact a disability rights organization or attorney.

  1. Document the missed deadline in writing Send an email to your school's special education coordinator noting the date the deadline passed and asking when the next step will happen. A written paper trail matters.
  2. Contact the principal or district special education director If the coordinator doesn't respond or resolve it, escalate to the principal. If still no action, contact the district's director of special education directly.
  3. File a state complaint Every state has a complaint process for IDEA violations. Your state's department of education must investigate within 60 days. This is free to file and you don't need a lawyer.
  4. File a complaint with the Office for Civil Rights (OCR) The OCR enforces Section 504 and the ADA. You can file a complaint at the U.S. Department of Education's website. There's no cost and you don't need an attorney.
  5. Request a due process hearing (IEP only) Under IDEA, you have the right to a formal due process hearing if you have a dispute about your IEP. This is the most formal and most adversarial option. Consider consulting a disability rights attorney before going this route.
