Use your voice

Knowing your rights is
step one. Using them is step two.

Self-advocacy is the skill of speaking up for your own needs. It's not about being confrontational; it's about being clear. Here's how to do it, with actual scripts.

Why it matters

What self-advocacy actually means

Self-advocacy means knowing what you need, being able to explain why you need it, and being willing to ask for it, even when it's uncomfortable. It doesn't mean being aggressive or demanding. It means being specific, calm, and persistent.

In high school, your parents or guardians can advocate alongside you. In college, you're on your own. The sooner you start practicing this skill, the easier that transition will be. Every conversation you have in high school where you speak up for yourself is practice.

The single most useful thing you can do: Know what's in your IEP or 504 Plan. Keep a copy. If you don't have one, ask your case manager or school counselor for it today. You cannot advocate for accommodations you don't know you have.

With your teachers

How to talk to teachers about your accommodations

Start at the beginning of each class: introduce your accommodations early and clearly. Don't wait until you're failing a test to bring up extended time. Use these scripts as a starting point and adjust them to how you talk.

At the start of the semester

"Hi, I wanted to let you know I have a 504 Plan [or IEP] that includes [extended time on tests / a quiet testing room / speech-to-text]. Can we set up a quick time to talk about how that works in your class?"

You don't have to explain your disability. You just need to introduce the accommodation and create a plan for how it'll work.

When a teacher doesn't follow your accommodation

"I noticed I didn't get my extended time on today's quiz. I have [extended time] listed in my plan; can we figure out how to make sure that happens going forward?"

Stay calm and specific. Assume it's an oversight first. If it happens again after you've brought it up, escalate.

When you need to use your accommodation in the moment

"I'm going to use my [break accommodation / move to the other room / type my answer], just letting you know."

You don't have to ask permission to use an accommodation that's already in your plan. State it, then do it.

When a teacher questions whether you really need it

"My IEP / 504 Plan documents this accommodation. If you have questions about it, my case manager is [name]. I'd be happy to bring a copy of my plan."

You don't have to justify your disability to a teacher. The plan is the documentation. Redirect to the official document.

In your IEP or 504 meeting

How to make the most of your meetings

IEP meetings can feel overwhelming: a room full of adults talking about you. But you're the most important person in that room. Your perspective matters, and you have the right to speak.

Before the meeting

  • Read your current IEP or 504 Plan before the meeting. Know what's in it.
  • Write down what's working and what's not. Be specific.
  • Write down what you want to add or change. Bring the list.
  • Think about what class, subject, or time of day is hardest for you.
  • Ask a parent, guardian, or trusted adult to attend with you if possible.

During the meeting

  • Introduce yourself and say you want to participate. "I'd like to share some things I've noticed this year."
  • If something isn't working, say so. "Extended time helps on long tests, but I still struggle with [specific thing]; can we talk about adding [specific accommodation]?"
  • If you don't understand something, ask. "Can you explain what that means in practice?"
  • If a decision is made that you disagree with, say so. "I'm not sure that's the right fit for me because [reason]. Can we discuss it?"
  • Ask for a copy of the final IEP. You are entitled to one.

Know your rights in meetings. You (or your parent/guardian) must consent to any changes to your IEP. No one can change your services without that consent. If you feel pressured to sign something you don't agree with, you can ask for more time to review it.

When things go wrong

When your school doesn't follow your plan

Your IEP or 504 Plan is a legal document. When your school doesn't follow it, that's a violation, not just a disappointment. Here's how to escalate, step by step.

Document everything. Every time an accommodation isn't provided, write it down: date, class, what happened, who was involved. This record matters if you need to escalate.

1

Talk to the teacher directly

Most of the time, a missed accommodation is an oversight. A calm, direct conversation ("My plan includes X, but it didn't happen today; can we fix that going forward?") resolves it. Do this in writing (email) so there's a record.

2

Contact your case manager or 504 coordinator

Your case manager (for IEPs) or 504 coordinator is responsible for making sure your plan is implemented. Email them, describe what happened, and ask them to address it with the teacher.

3

Contact the principal

If your case manager doesn't resolve it, escalate to the principal. Send an email summarizing the issue, what you've done, and what you're asking for. Keep it factual.

4

Contact the district's director of special education

Each school district has a special education director. They oversee all special education services in the district and have authority over building-level staff. A formal complaint to this person usually gets results.

5

File a state complaint or OCR complaint

If the district doesn't resolve it, file a formal complaint. Your state's department of education handles IDEA complaints. The U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights handles Section 504 and ADA complaints. Both are free and don't require a lawyer. See the Resources page for links.

Keep your records

What to keep and why

Good documentation protects you. Keep these things in a folder (physical or digital) somewhere you can find them.

  • A copy of your current IEP or 504 Plan. You are entitled to one. Ask for it if you don't have it.
  • All written communications with teachers, administrators, and school staff about your accommodations.
  • A log of any time an accommodation wasn't provided: date, class, what happened.
  • Evaluation reports from any school-based or independent evaluations.
  • Your Summary of Performance (IEP students only; ask for this before you graduate, as it's yours by right).
