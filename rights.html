Federal law is on your side

Three laws.
All on your side.

IDEA, Section 504, and the ADA all protect students with disabilities in public K-12 schools. Most students have never heard of any of them. Here's what they actually say.

Before you read anything else

Two things every student needs to know

You do NOT need a diagnosis to request an evaluation. You have the right to ask your school to evaluate you for a disability. The school cannot require you to get diagnosed on your own first. They must evaluate you if they have reason to believe a disability might be affecting your education.

A written request starts the clock. A verbal request does not. If you ask your teacher or principal out loud, nothing official starts. You need to put it in writing: an email, a letter, anything written. That's when the legal timelines begin. See a sample request letter.

This is not legal advice. This page explains federal law in plain English. Your state may have additional protections. If you have a serious dispute, contact a disability rights attorney or organization.

The three laws

What the law actually says

These three federal laws work together. Most students with disabilities in K-12 schools are covered by at least two of them.

1

IDEA: Individuals with Disabilities Education Act

20 U.S.C. § 1400 et seq.

IDEA is the strongest protection for students with disabilities. It guarantees students the right to a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) in the Least Restrictive Environment (LRE) possible. That means your school must educate you with students who don't have disabilities whenever it's appropriate to do so, not just shut you into a separate room.

IDEA covers students from birth through age 21 (or high school graduation, whichever comes first). It requires schools to develop an IEP for every eligible student and follow it.

  • FAPE means your school must provide an education that actually meets your unique needs, not just place you in a classroom and hope for the best.
  • LRE means you have the right to be educated alongside your non-disabled peers as much as is appropriate for your situation.
  • IDEA covers 13 specific disability categories, including learning disabilities, ADHD (under Other Health Impairment), autism, emotional disturbance, speech/language impairments, and physical disabilities.
  • Your school must provide an annual IEP meeting and must re-evaluate you at least every three years.
  • Parents and students (once old enough) have the right to participate in all IEP decisions.
2

Section 504: Rehabilitation Act of 1973

29 U.S.C. § 794

Section 504 is a civil rights law that prohibits discrimination against people with disabilities in any program that receives federal funding, including your public school. It has a broader definition of disability than IDEA, which means many students who don't qualify for an IEP can still get a 504 Plan.

Under Section 504, a disability is any physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, including learning, reading, concentrating, thinking, communicating, or walking.

  • Section 504 doesn't require specialized instruction; it requires your school to give you equal access to education through accommodations.
  • There's no exhaustive list of conditions that qualify. If your condition substantially limits a major life activity, you may be eligible.
  • Students who don't qualify for IDEA (because they don't need specialized instruction) often qualify under Section 504.
  • Section 504 has fewer procedural protections than IDEA; your school has more flexibility in how it implements the plan.
3

ADA: Americans with Disabilities Act

42 U.S.C. § 12101 et seq.

The ADA prohibits discrimination against people with disabilities in public life, including public schools. In K-12, the ADA largely overlaps with Section 504 (both are enforced by the Office for Civil Rights). But the ADA is broader: it applies even to schools that don't receive federal funding, and it gives you the right to file a discrimination complaint if your school is treating you unfairly because of your disability.

  • The ADA defines disability very broadly: any physical or mental impairment that substantially limits a major life activity.
  • In K-12, the ADA and Section 504 often work together. If one applies, so does the other.
  • The ADA also protects you from retaliation if you advocate for your rights.

What you're entitled to

Your specific rights

These rights flow from the laws above. Your school is legally required to honor them.

Right to an Evaluation

You can request a free evaluation by your school at any time. If your school suspects you have a disability that affects your education, they must evaluate you even if you don't ask.

Right to FAPE

Under IDEA, your education must be free and appropriate: designed for your specific needs, not just whatever is convenient for the school.

Right to Prior Written Notice

Your school must notify you (and your parents) in writing before making any significant change to your educational placement or services, including refusing to make a change you requested.

Right to Participate in Your IEP

Once you're old enough, you have the right to attend and participate in your IEP meetings. You don't have to just sit there; you can and should speak up.

Right to Dispute Decisions

If you disagree with your school's evaluation or a decision about your services, you have the right to challenge it through mediation, a due process hearing, or a complaint to the Office for Civil Rights.

Right to an Independent Evaluation

If you disagree with your school's evaluation, you can request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at your school's expense, or pay for one yourself and have your school consider the results.

Right to Confidentiality

Your disability-related records are protected under FERPA. Your school cannot share them without your consent (or your parents' consent if you're under 18).

Right to Protection from Retaliation

The ADA protects you if you advocate for your rights. Your school cannot punish you or treat you worse because you asked for accommodations or filed a complaint.
