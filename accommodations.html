What you can ask for

You can ask for these.
Your school must consider them.

Accommodations are changes to how you access your education — not changes to what you're expected to learn. Here's what exists, who it helps, and how to bring it up.

Know the difference

Accommodations vs. modifications

These two words get used interchangeably — but they mean different things, and understanding the difference matters.

Accommodation

Changes how you access the material — without changing what you're expected to learn or the standards you're held to. Extended time on a test is an accommodation. You're still expected to know the same content.

Modification

Changes what you're expected to learn or the standard you're held to. Fewer test questions covering less content is a modification. Modifications can affect grades and transcript — ask your school specifically before agreeing to any.

Most students with 504 Plans get accommodations only. Students with IEPs may have both, depending on their needs. If your school suggests a modification, ask how it will appear on your transcript and whether it affects your diploma track before agreeing.

This is not legal advice. Specific accommodations must be determined through the IEP or 504 process, based on your individual evaluation. Not every accommodation is appropriate for every student.

What's out there

Common accommodations by category

This isn't an exhaustive list — there are many more. Think about where you struggle and look for accommodations that address the barrier, not just the symptom.

Testing accommodations

Extended time

Extra time to complete tests — typically 1.5x or 2x the standard time. Common for ADHD, anxiety, processing speed differences, and fine motor delays.

Separate testing room

Taking tests in a quieter, less distracting environment. Helpful for attention and anxiety disorders.

Reader (human or text-to-speech)

Test questions read aloud to you. Used for dyslexia, visual impairments, and some processing disorders.

Scribe or speech-to-text for written answers

A person or software records your spoken answers. Common for fine motor delays, dysgraphia, and physical disabilities.

Calculator on non-math tests

Use of a calculator on tests that aren't measuring calculation ability. Often part of a learning disability accommodation.

Breaks during testing

Scheduled breaks without the clock stopping. Helpful for attention, anxiety, and chronic illness.

Test read multiple times

Questions can be re-read as often as needed. Used for auditory processing or attention differences.

Large print or modified format

Tests printed in larger font or on different paper. Common for visual impairments and some processing differences.

Instruction accommodations

Preferential seating

Sitting closer to the front, away from distractions, or near the door. Useful for attention and sensory differences.

Copies of teacher notes or slides

Receiving the teacher's notes before or after class. Helpful when handwriting speed, attention, or fine motor skills make note-taking difficult.

Extended time on assignments

Extra days to complete homework or projects. Common for executive function challenges, anxiety, and processing differences.

Reduced homework load

Fewer questions covering the same concepts. Appropriate when a student has demonstrated understanding but production is a barrier.

Oral instead of written responses

Answering questions aloud rather than in writing. Used for dysgraphia, fine motor delays, or students with severe writing anxiety.

Chunked assignments

Long tasks broken into smaller steps with separate due dates. Helpful for executive function and attention challenges.

Technology accommodations

Speech-to-text software

Dictate your writing instead of typing. Used for fine motor delays, dysgraphia, and physical disabilities.

Text-to-speech software

Have written text read aloud. Used for dyslexia, visual impairments, and auditory learners with reading challenges.

Word processor for written work

Typing instead of handwriting for assignments and tests. Common for fine motor delays and dysgraphia.

Spell-check or grammar tools

Use of assistive spelling software. Often allowed for dyslexia when the test isn't measuring spelling ability.

Audiobooks or read-aloud versions

Textbooks and reading materials in audio form. Used for dyslexia, visual impairments, and reading processing differences.

Environment and sensory accommodations

Movement breaks

Scheduled breaks to move around during class. Common for ADHD and sensory processing differences.

Fidget tools

Use of approved sensory tools during class. Helps students with ADHD, autism, and sensory processing needs maintain focus.

Standing desk or alternative seating

Flexibility in how and where you sit. Used for ADHD, sensory differences, and some physical conditions.

Noise-reducing headphones

Wearing noise-canceling or hearing protection during class or tests. Common for sensory processing disorders and anxiety.

Reduced sensory load

Adjustments to lighting, seating arrangements, or classroom setup. Used for sensory processing differences and autism spectrum conditions.

Physical and motor accommodations

Extra time between classes

Additional passing time to move between classrooms. Used for physical disabilities, mobility impairments, and chronic illness.

Modified PE or adapted physical education

Adjusted physical education requirements or activities. Common for physical disabilities and some chronic health conditions.

Elevator access or accessible routes

Access to elevators and accessible pathways in school buildings.

Assistive devices in class

Use of mobility aids, grip aids, slant boards, or other physical assistive devices during instruction.

Reduced fine motor tasks

Alternatives to tasks requiring precise hand movements, like cutting, drawing, or handwriting. Used for fine motor delays.

How to get what you need

How to ask for accommodations that fit you

When you're requesting accommodations, describe your functional barrier — not just your label. "I have ADHD" is less useful than "I run out of time on tests because I re-read questions multiple times before I can process them." Here's how to make a strong case.

  • 1
    Name the barrier, not the diagnosis Describe specifically what's hard. "I have trouble organizing my thoughts while handwriting fast" is more useful than "I have anxiety." The accommodation needs to address the barrier.
  • 2
    Connect the barrier to the accommodation "Because I have a fine motor delay that slows my handwriting, I need to use a word processor on exams" is a clear, logical connection. Make it obvious why the accommodation addresses your specific need.
  • 3
    Bring it to your IEP or 504 meeting Your accommodations are decided in your plan meetings — not in individual conversations with teachers. Bring a list of what you want and ask for it to be added to your plan document.
  • 4
    Get it in writing An accommodation only exists if it's in your plan. A teacher saying "sure, I'll give you extra time" is not a formal accommodation. Make sure it's documented.

Now let's talk about using them.

Knowing your accommodations is one thing. Getting your teachers to actually follow them is another. Learn how to advocate for yourself — in class, in meetings, and when the school doesn't follow through.
