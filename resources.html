Official sources

U.S. government resources

These are the authoritative sources for disability law in education. When you need to verify a legal claim or find official guidance, start here.

This is not legal advice. These resources are provided for informational purposes. For advice specific to your situation, contact a disability rights organization or attorney.

U.S. Department of Education — Office for Civil Rights (OCR)

The OCR enforces Section 504 and the ADA in schools. You can file a complaint here if your school is discriminating against you or failing to provide required accommodations. Complaints are free and you don't need a lawyer.

File an OCR complaint →

OSERS — Office of Special Education and Rehabilitative Services

The federal office that administers IDEA. Their website has policy guidance documents, Q&A publications, and resources explaining how IDEA works in practice.

IDEA website (sites.ed.gov/idea) →

U.S. Department of Education — ed.gov

The main Department of Education website. Contains publications on student rights, disability rights in education, and official policy documents.

Special Education guidance (ed.gov) →

ADA National Network

A federally funded network of regional centers that provide information, guidance, and training on the Americans with Disabilities Act. Free phone and email support available.

adata.org →

Organizations that can help

Student and family organizations

These organizations offer advocacy support, legal help, and plain-language resources for students and families navigating the disability rights system.

PACER Center

Parent Advocacy Coalition for Educational Rights. Provides resources, workshops, and legal training for parents and students with disabilities. Their Student Training on Awareness of Rights (STAR) program is specifically designed for students.

pacer.org →

Understood.org

One of the best plain-language resources for students with learning and attention issues — ADHD, dyslexia, dyscalculia, and more. Includes guides to 504 Plans, IEPs, school rights, and self-advocacy. Written for both parents and students.

understood.org →

COPAA — Council of Parent Attorneys and Advocates

Protects and enforces the legal and civil rights of students with disabilities. Their attorney directory can help you find a special education attorney in your area if you need legal representation.

copaa.org →

National Center for Learning Disabilities (NCLD)

Advocates for the 1 in 5 students with learning and attention issues. Their State of LD report tracks how schools are serving students with disabilities nationally. Also provides policy resources and action guides.

ncld.org →

Parent Training and Information (PTI) Centers

Every state has a federally funded PTI Center that provides free training and information to parents and students about their rights under IDEA and Section 504. They can help you understand your rights, prepare for IEP meetings, and resolve disputes with your school.

Find your state's PTI Center →

Use these right now

Sample request letters

Copy these, fill in the highlighted sections, and send them via email or certified mail. Keep a copy. These are starting points — adjust the language to fit your situation.

Important: Send all formal requests in writing. Email counts — just keep a copy and note the date. Verbal requests do not start any legal timelines.

Letter 1: Request for initial evaluation

Use this to formally request that your school evaluate your child (or yourself, if you're 18+) for a disability. This is the letter that starts the official process and the legal clock.

[Today's date]

Dear [Principal's name or Special Education Coordinator's name],

I am writing to formally request that [School name] conduct a full and individual evaluation of my [son/daughter/child], [Student's full name], currently enrolled in grade [grade], to determine whether they have a disability that qualifies them for special education services or related services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and/or Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973.

I am requesting this evaluation because [briefly describe what you're observing — e.g., "my child is struggling significantly with reading and attention in class, and these difficulties appear to be affecting their academic performance and ability to access the curriculum"].

Please send me written acknowledgment of this request, along with information about the next steps, the timeline, and my rights as a parent in this process.

Thank you for your attention to this matter.

Sincerely,
[Your name]
[Your phone number and email]
[Student's name and grade]

Letter 2: Complaint that plan isn't being followed

Use this when your IEP or 504 Plan is in place but not being followed. Send it to the case manager or principal first. Escalate if there's no response within a week.

[Today's date]

Dear [Case manager's name / Principal's name],

I am writing to bring to your attention a concern regarding the implementation of [my son's/my daughter's/my] [IEP / 504 Plan] at [School name].

Specifically, the following accommodation(s) listed in the plan have not been consistently provided:

[Describe the specific accommodation(s) not being followed — e.g., "Extended time on tests (1.5x) has not been provided in Ms. Smith's class on the following dates: October 3, October 15, and October 22, 2024."]

This accommodation is documented in the plan dated [date of plan]. As you know, the school is legally required to implement the plan as written.

I am requesting that you take immediate steps to ensure this accommodation is provided as documented, and that you confirm in writing how this will be addressed going forward. Please respond within [5 / 7] school days.

If this issue is not resolved, I will need to contact the district's Director of Special Education and, if necessary, file a complaint with the Office for Civil Rights.

Thank you for your prompt attention.

Sincerely,
[Your name]
[Your phone number and email]
[Student's name and grade]

Letter 3: Request for Summary of Performance

For IEP students approaching graduation. Send this to your case manager or special education coordinator before your final semester.

[Today's date]

Dear [Case manager's name],

I am writing to request a Summary of Performance (SOP) for [Student's full name], who is expected to graduate from [School name] in [month/year].

As required under IDEA (20 U.S.C. § 1414(c)(5)(B)(ii)), the school must provide a Summary of Performance when a student's eligibility for special education terminates due to graduation with a regular diploma. I understand this document should summarize academic achievement, functional performance, and recommendations for supporting postsecondary needs.

I would appreciate it if the SOP could be as specific as possible, including information about functional limitations and the accommodations that have been effective, as this documentation will be helpful when registering with college disability services.

Please let me know when I can expect to receive this document.

Thank you.

Sincerely,
[Your name]
[Your phone number and email]
[Student's name and expected graduation date]

Don't forget state resources

Your state has resources too

Federal law sets the floor — many states have stronger protections or additional requirements. Your state also has free, locally accessible resources.

Find your state's Parent Training and Information (PTI) Center

Every state has a federally funded PTI Center that offers free help to families and students — training workshops, one-on-one support, help preparing for IEP meetings, and assistance filing complaints. This is often the fastest path to local, personalized help.

Find your state's PTI Center →

Your state's department of education

Each state's department of education has a special education division that handles complaints about IDEA violations and can provide state-specific guidance. Search "[your state] department of education special education" to find your state's office.

Protection and Advocacy (P&A) organizations

Every state has a federally mandated Protection and Advocacy organization that provides legal advocacy services to people with disabilities — including students in K-12. Services are often free or low-cost.

Find your state's P&A agency →
