Frequently asked questions

What students want to know

This is not legal advice. These answers reflect federal U.S. law. State laws and individual school policies vary. For serious disputes, consult a disability rights organization or attorney.

No. You do not need a diagnosis to request an evaluation.

You — or your parent or guardian — have the right to request a free evaluation from your school at any time. Your school cannot require you to have an existing diagnosis before they evaluate you. In fact, the purpose of the evaluation is to determine whether you have a disability that's affecting your education.

All that's needed is a reason to suspect that a disability might be affecting your ability to learn. Your school also has an independent obligation called "Child Find" to identify students who may need support — even if no one requests an evaluation.

Put your request in writing. That's when the legal process begins. See a sample request letter.

Yes, but only in writing — and they must explain why and tell you about your rights.

If your school decides not to evaluate you, they must send you a written notice explaining their decision and their reasoning. This is called Prior Written Notice. They must also inform you of your right to challenge the decision.

If you believe the denial was wrong, you can:

  • Request mediation with the school district
  • File a state complaint with your state's department of education
  • Request a due process hearing (for IDEA) or file a complaint with the Office for Civil Rights (for Section 504)

A school that simply says "no" verbally without written notice is not following the law.

You have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE).

If you disagree with the school's evaluation, you can request that the school pay for an independent evaluation by a qualified professional outside the school. This is called an IEE at public expense.

The school can either agree to fund the IEE or file for a due process hearing to defend their own evaluation. If they file for due process and the hearing officer agrees with the school's evaluation, you still have the right to get an independent evaluation — you'd just pay for it yourself.

Your school must consider the results of any IEE you get, whether it was paid for by them or by you.

No. A 504 Plan has fewer procedural protections than an IEP.

Both plans give you the right to accommodations. But IEPs come with significantly stronger legal protections, including:

  • Strict federal timelines for evaluations and plan development
  • Annual review requirements
  • The right to a due process hearing if you disagree with a decision
  • More detailed requirements for what must be in the plan
  • The right to an independent evaluation at public expense

A 504 Plan still gives you legal recourse — through the Office for Civil Rights — but the process is less formal and there are fewer specific federal requirements your school must follow. Read the full comparison.

Start by talking to the teacher directly — in writing, so there's a record. Many missed accommodations are oversights. Keep the conversation calm and specific: "My plan includes X — can we make sure that happens going forward?"

If it continues, escalate: contact your case manager or 504 coordinator, then the principal, then the district's special education director. If it still isn't resolved, you can file a state complaint (for IDEA violations) or a complaint with the Office for Civil Rights (for Section 504 violations).

Document every instance with dates, classes, and what happened. That record matters if you need to escalate. See the full escalation guide.

That is not a legal excuse. Lack of budget does not exempt a school from its legal obligations.

Under IDEA, your school is required to provide a Free Appropriate Public Education regardless of cost. Under Section 504, schools cannot deny accommodations because they cost money. Schools receive federal funding specifically to support students with disabilities.

If your school is using budget as a reason to deny services that are required by your IEP or 504 Plan, document the conversation and escalate. This is a violation of federal law.

No. Nothing transfers automatically.

Your IEP ends at high school graduation. It is not enforceable in college. Your 504 Plan also doesn't transfer — though Section 504 and the ADA still apply in college, you must re-register with your college's disability services office from scratch.

You'll need to provide documentation, go through an intake process, and re-request your accommodations every semester at most colleges. No one will come looking for you — you have to initiate everything. Read the full college transition guide.

The Summary of Performance (SOP) is a document your school is required to give IEP students when their IEP ends — at graduation or when you age out. It summarizes your academic achievement, your functional abilities, and recommendations for supporting your needs in postsecondary settings.

Most students have never heard of it. But if you have an IEP, you are entitled to one.

It's not automatically handed to you — ask your case manager before graduation. Ask that it be detailed and specific about your functional needs and the accommodations that helped. It can be useful when registering with college disability services. Learn more about the college transition.

No. Eligibility for special education is not based on your grades.

IEP eligibility is based on whether you have a qualifying disability and whether that disability affects your educational performance in a way that requires special education services. If you're passing your classes because of your accommodations and services, that doesn't mean you no longer need them.

Your school cannot remove your IEP just because your grades are good. They can only change your eligibility determination through a formal reevaluation process — and you (or your parent/guardian) must be notified and can challenge the decision.

The Office for Civil Rights (OCR) is a division of the U.S. Department of Education that enforces federal civil rights laws in schools — including Section 504 and the ADA. If your school discriminates against you because of your disability, or fails to provide accommodations required under Section 504, you can file a complaint with the OCR.

You don't need a lawyer to file. It's free. The OCR will investigate and, if they find a violation, require the school to take corrective action.

When to contact OCR:

  • Your school refuses to evaluate you for a disability
  • Your school denies accommodations without a valid reason
  • Your school consistently fails to follow your 504 Plan
  • You believe you're being discriminated against because of your disability

Exhaust your school-level options first (teacher, case manager, principal, district). OCR is a last resort, but it's a real and effective one. Find the OCR complaint link on the Resources page.
