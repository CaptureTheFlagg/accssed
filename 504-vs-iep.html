Understanding your options

Two plans.
One is right for you.

A 504 Plan and an IEP are not interchangeable. They come from different laws, cover different students, and offer different levels of support. Here's what you actually need to know.

The basics

What is each one?

Both plans are designed to help students with disabilities succeed in school — but they work very differently. Understanding the difference helps you ask for the right one.

504 Plan

Section 504 Plan

Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973

  • Provides accommodations only — changes to how you access your education
  • Does not include specialized instruction or additional services
  • No required meetings, timelines, or formal written plan (though most schools do write one)
  • Covers any student whose disability substantially limits a major life activity — including learning, reading, concentrating, or walking
  • Easier to qualify for, but fewer legal protections if the school doesn't follow through
IEP

Individualized Education Program

Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)

  • Provides accommodations plus specialized instruction, related services (speech therapy, OT, etc.), and goals
  • Requires a formal written document updated at least annually
  • Has strict federal timelines and procedural rights
  • Covers students in 13 specific disability categories under IDEA
  • More protections — your school must follow the IEP or you have legal recourse

Important: ADHD is not its own category under IDEA. Students with ADHD typically qualify under Other Health Impairment (OHI) — which is one of IDEA's 13 categories. Don't let anyone tell you ADHD doesn't qualify for an IEP. It can.

Side by side

How they compare

This table breaks down the key differences between a 504 Plan and an IEP.

Category 504 Plan IEP
Governing law Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)
Who qualifies Any student whose disability substantially limits a major life activity Students in one of IDEA's 13 disability categories who need specialized instruction
What you get Accommodations only (e.g., extended time, preferential seating) Accommodations + specialized instruction + related services + annual goals
Written document required Not federally required, though most schools provide one Yes — legally required, detailed written plan
Federal timelines No fixed federal timeline — "reasonable time" standard Yes — 60 days to evaluate, 30 days to develop IEP after evaluation
Annual review Not required (though recommended) Yes — required annually
Parental rights Section 504 requires prior written notice and the right to an impartial hearing to dispute decisions — but these procedural protections are less detailed than those under IDEA. Extensive (right to be part of the team, prior written notice, dispute resolution, independent evaluations)
Transfers to college No — college is a different system entirely No — college is a different system entirely
Ends when When you graduate or leave the school Permanently ends at high school graduation

This is not legal advice. These descriptions reflect federal law. Your state may have additional protections. If you have a dispute with your school, contact a disability rights organization.

Which one fits you?

How to think about this

The right plan depends on what you actually need. Here's a simple way to think through it. These are guidelines — your school's evaluation team makes the final determination.

  • You need changes to how tests are given or how class is set up Consider 504 If extended time, a quiet room, or copies of notes would handle it, a 504 may be enough.
  • You need different instruction — not just more time Consider IEP If you need a teacher to teach you differently, break subjects into smaller steps, or provide reading support, that's specialized instruction — only an IEP covers this.
  • You need related services like speech therapy, occupational therapy, or counseling Consider IEP Section 504 does not include these. IDEA does.
  • Your disability is mild and your grades are mostly on track Consider 504 If your main barrier is access — not instruction — a 504 is often faster to get and still legally enforceable.
  • You want stronger legal protections and formal documentation Consider IEP IEPs come with significantly more procedural rights. If your school has a history of not following through, the IEP's legal framework gives you more leverage.

You don't have to pick. You can request that the school evaluate you — and the evaluation team will determine which plan, if any, is appropriate. You can ask for a specific plan, but the school's team makes the final call. If you disagree with their decision, you have the right to dispute it.
